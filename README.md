# Provenance Guard

A backend content attribution system that classifies submitted text as AI-generated 
or human-written, scores confidence, surfaces transparency labels, and handles creator appeals.

## Architecture Overview

A submitted piece of text travels through the following path:

1. Client sends POST /submit with `text` and `creator_id`
2. A unique `content_id` is generated for the submission
3. Text is sent to Groq LLM → returns `llm_score` (0–1)
4. Text is analyzed with stylometric heuristics → returns `stylometric_score` (0–1)
5. Spread is checked — if `abs(llm_score - stylometric_score) > 0.30`, result is forced uncertain
6. Otherwise, weighted average is computed: 40% LLM + 60% stylometric → `confidence`
7. Confidence is mapped to a transparency label
8. Entry is written to audit log
9. Response returned to client with `content_id`, `attribution`, `confidence`, and `label`

Appeal flow:
1. Client sends POST /appeal with `content_id` and `creator_reasoning`
2. Original submission is looked up by `content_id`
3. Status updated to `under_review`
4. Appeal reasoning appended to audit log entry
5. Confirmation returned to client

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1: LLM Classification (Groq)
- **What it measures:** Holistic semantic and stylistic assessment of whether text reads as AI-generated
- **Output:** A score between 0–1 where 1 = definitely AI, 0 = definitely human
- **Why chosen:** LLMs capture meaning, tone, and stylistic coherence that pure statistics miss
- **Blind spots:** Polished, formal human writing (academic essays, structured arguments) can pattern-match to AI writing and score high incorrectly. The LLM is the signal more likely to produce false positives.

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics
- **What it measures:** Statistical properties — sentence length variance and type-token ratio (vocabulary diversity)
- **Output:** A score between 0–1 where 1 = definitely AI, 0 = definitely human
- **Why chosen:** Structural properties are independent of semantics — they catch AI uniformity that the LLM might miss on lightly edited outputs
- **Blind spots:** Poetry (short, repetitive lines by artistic choice) and non-native English speakers (careful, uniform sentence structure) both produce low variance that stylometrics incorrectly flags as AI-like

### Combining Signals
Weights: 60% stylometric, 40% LLM. The LLM is more likely to confidently label something AI-generated, so weighting stylometrics slightly higher reduces false positive risk. When signals disagree by more than 0.30, the result is forced to uncertain regardless of individual scores.

## Confidence Scoring

Scores are combined as: `confidence = (0.4 × llm_score) + (0.6 × stylometric_score)`

Thresholds:
- **≥ 0.75** → Likely AI
- **0.45 – 0.74** → Uncertain
- **< 0.45** → Likely Human

The asymmetry is intentional — false positives (calling human work AI) are worse than false negatives on a creative platform. The high bar of 0.75 to call something AI reflects that cost. The lower bar of 0.45 to call something human is more forgiving by design.

### Example Submissions

**Casual human text (signals agreed, high confidence human):**
```json
{
  "text": "ok so i finally tried that new ramen place downtown and honestly? underwhelming...",
  "llm_score": 0.1,
  "stylometric_score": 0.154,
  "confidence": 0.132,
  "attribution": "likely_human"
}
```
Both signals agreed (spread 0.054) → weighted average applied → confidently human.

**Formal AI-style text (signals disagreed, forced uncertain):**
```json
{
  "text": "Artificial intelligence represents a transformative paradigm shift...",
  "llm_score": 0.8,
  "stylometric_score": 0.171,
  "confidence": 0.423,
  "attribution": "uncertain"
}
```
Spread of 0.629 exceeded the 0.30 threshold → forced uncertain.

## Rate Limiting

Limits: **10 requests per minute, 100 requests per day** per IP address.

Reasoning: A legitimate creator submitting their own work would rarely need more than a few submissions per minute — even accounting for revisions and retests. 10 per minute is generous for normal use while blocking automated flooding. 100 per day prevents sustained abuse across a full day while still accommodating heavy legitimate users.

## Known Limitations

**Short texts with formal vocabulary:** The stylometric signal performs poorly on short submissions. With only 2–3 sentences, sentence length variance is inherently low regardless of whether the author is human or AI, pushing scores toward "AI-like." A skilled human writer producing a short, formal paragraph will frequently trigger uncertain or false AI classifications.

**Signal disagreement rate:** Because the LLM and stylometric signals measure fundamentally different properties, they disagree frequently — producing a spread over 0.30 and forcing uncertain even on clearly AI-generated text. This means the system errs heavily toward uncertainty, which is safe but reduces its usefulness as a definitive classifier.

## Spec Reflection

**Where the spec helped:** The explicit warning about false positives directly shaped the confidence scoring design — the asymmetric thresholds (0.75 to call AI, 0.45 to call human) and the decision to weight stylometrics at 60% both came from thinking through that constraint carefully before writing any code.

**Where implementation diverged:** The planning doc assumed the stylometric signal would be a reliable counterbalance to the LLM signal. In practice, the two signals disagreed so frequently that the spread threshold forced "uncertain" on most submissions — including clearly AI-generated text. If rebuilding, I would recalibrate the spread threshold or add a third signal to break ties.
